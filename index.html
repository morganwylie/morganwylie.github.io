Introduction


If you have ever wanted to read 'The Iliad' but have been intimidated by its large size and difficult dialect, look no further than this guide! A comprehensive description of everything you might want to know before picking up the book.

Before beginning, there are some facts that you need to know to be able to understand this website and any further discussions of the poem;


The Epic Cycle


The epic cycle refers to the collection of poems that transcribe the events of the Trojan war. The Iliad is believed to be the second poem and the Odyssey the second last.


Modifications


In the iliad there are a few different language choices that might confuse new readers, the most notably being that the Greeks are not referred to as such. They are called the Achaeans. Similarly the poem is split into books, not chapters.


Translations


The original poem is written in an ancient Greek language, and has been translated hundreds of times. These different translations have the same major plot point but differ in the subtleties. If you want to read it as close to the original as possible, translations like Lattimore and Wilson are often referenced as relatively faithful. Its difficulties come with the language used, difficult and irregular. If you just want to enjoy the story and do not worry too much about the accuracy, translations like Fagles may be for you, but these simpler versions often lack the same depth and metaphorical language as others. So it is important to know what type of experience you want from The Iliad before choosing a translation.


With all this settled, now you can continue through this website or the Iliad itself.
